Drug Discovery: Context

NCX-1015 provides us with a real-world case study to understand the drug discovery pipeline.

Where does NCX-1015 sit?

  • ✅ Target identification (GR / inflammation)
  • ✅ Target validation (known GC pharmacology)
  • ✅ Lead identification & optimisation (10 compounds → NCX-1015)
  • ✅ Preclinical testing (in vitro + in vivo models)
  • ❓ Clinical candidate selection
  • ❓ Clinical trials

The Drug Discovery Cycle

Core Iterative Loop

SAR = Structure-Activity Relationship

The cycle is iterative — compounds are continuously refined based on pharmacological and metabolic data.

Traditional Drug Discovery Timeline

Stage Duration Cumulative
Target Identification 0.5–1 year ~1 year
Target Validation 0.5–1 year ~2 years
Lead Identification 0.5–1 year ~3 years
Candidate Optimisation 2–3 years ~5–6 years

Important

The traditional pipeline from target to market takes 12–14 years on average.

Drug Reprofiling: A Shortcut

SWITCH Drug Re-Profiling Approach

Instead of starting from scratch:

  1. Begin at Lead Identification stage with an existing known compound
  2. Use data mining of existing pharmacological, safety, and clinical data
  3. Skip target identification, validation, and much of preclinical work
  4. Move directly to Clinical Proof of Concept trials

Timeline: 0.5–1 year (data mining) → Clinical trial

Tip

NCX-1015 is essentially a reprofiling strategy — starting with a known drug (prednisolone) and chemically modifying it to improve the therapeutic profile while leveraging existing knowledge of GC pharmacology.

The “Valley of Death”

The transition from pre-clinical to Phase I is called the translation gap or “valley of death” — where many promising preclinical findings fail to translate to human efficacy.

Why Do So Many Products Fail?

Key Reasons for Attrition

  • Lack of efficacy in humans (despite preclinical success)
  • Toxicity not predicted by animal models
  • Poor pharmacokinetics (ADMET failures)
  • Commercial viability reassessment
  • Manufacturing challenges at scale

The Academic–Industry Interface

Drug discovery increasingly relies on collaboration between:

  • Academic researchers (target identification, biological mechanism, animal models)
  • Academic drug discovery institutes (HTS, medicinal chemistry, lead optimisation)
  • Industry partners (ADMET, scale-up, clinical trials)
  • Supported by federal funding, foundations, and philanthropic groups

Summary: Part 5

  • The drug discovery pipeline spans 12–14 years from target to market
  • Only 1 in 10,000–20,000 compounds reaches approval
  • Total capitalised cost exceeds $1.6 billion per approved drug
  • The translation gap (valley of death) between preclinical and clinical stages is where most programmes fail
  • Drug reprofiling offers a faster, cheaper route by leveraging existing compound data
  • NCX-1015 represents a successful preclinical drug discovery programme that addressed a genuine clinical unmet need
  • The compound demonstrates how academic–industry collaboration drives innovation

Self-Assessment

Q1: At what stage does the highest probability of attrition occur in the drug discovery pipeline, and why? Phase II clinical trials have the highest attrition rate at 56%. This is because Phase II is the first time a compound is tested for efficacy in the target patient population. Many compounds that show promise in preclinical models and are safe in Phase I (healthy volunteers) fail to demonstrate sufficient therapeutic benefit in actual patients — the translation gap.
Q2: How does NCX-1015 exemplify a drug reprofiling approach? NCX-1015 starts with prednisolone, a well-established drug with decades of clinical data on efficacy, safety, pharmacokinetics, and side effects. Rather than discovering an entirely new compound, the researchers chemically modified an existing drug to improve its therapeutic profile. This leverages existing GC knowledge, bypasses early target identification/validation stages, and accelerates the path to clinical testing.
Q3: What additional studies would NCX-1015 need before entering Phase I clinical trials? Key studies would include: formal toxicology (acute and chronic, in two species), detailed pharmacokinetics (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion), reproductive toxicology, genotoxicity assessment, GMP-grade manufacturing process development, and preparation of an Investigational New Drug (IND) application or equivalent regulatory submission.
