Harvey William Cushing (1869–1939)

  • American neurosurgeon and endocrinologist
  • Pioneer of brain surgery techniques
  • First described the clinical syndrome of cortisol excess
  • Linked pituitary basophil adenomas to the constellation of symptoms now bearing his name

Cushing’s Syndrome: Overview

Definition: Clinical syndrome resulting from excessive exposure to glucocorticoids

Prevalence: 10–15 million sufferers worldwide

Three Major Causes

Cause Mechanism
Iatrogenic Prolonged exogenous GC treatment
Adrenal tumour Autonomous cortisol secretion
Excess CRH/ACTH Pituitary adenoma or ectopic ACTH

Important

Fatal if untreated — but treatable with adrenalectomy and glucocorticoid replacement therapy.

Three Major Endogenous Causes

Note

Cushing’s disease specifically refers to the pituitary adenoma cause; Cushing’s syndrome is the broader term for any cause of cortisol excess.

  • Hypertension — 85%

  • Proximal weakness — 85%

Psychiatric (85%)

  • Emotional lability
  • Euphoria

Metabolic

Addison’s Disease: Overview

  • Thomas Addison (1855) — first described adrenal insufficiency
  • Hypoproduction of cortisol
  • Difficult to diagnose clinically — insidious onset, non-specific symptoms
  • Treated with lifelong glucocorticoid replacement

Addison’s Disease: Symptoms

Chronic Symptoms

  • Extreme fatigue
  • Weight loss & decreased appetite
  • Hyperpigmentation (primary only)
  • Low blood pressure / fainting
  • Salt craving
  • Hypoglycaemia
  • GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea)
  • Abdominal, muscle & joint pain

Neuropsychiatric

  • Irritability
  • Depression
  • Behavioural changes

Other

  • Body hair loss in women
  • Sexual dysfunction

Addisonian Crisis ⚠️

  • Severe hypotension
  • Hyperkalaemia
  • Life-threatening emergency

Primary vs Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency

Primary (Addison’s)

Adrenal gland destroyed

  • Autoimmune adrenalitis (commonest in UK)
  • Tuberculosis
  • Other infections
  • Metastatic cancer
  • Adrenal haemorrhage

→ Hyperpigmentation PRESENT

Secondary

Pituitary/hypothalamic failure

  • Benign pituitary tumours
  • Pituitary inflammation
  • Prior pituitary surgery
  • Inappropriate corticosteroid withdrawal

→ NO hyperpigmentation

Why Hyperpigmentation in Primary Disease?

  • In primary disease, ACTH is elevated because the adrenal cannot respond
  • ACTH is co-derived from POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin)
  • ACTH and related peptides activate melanocortin receptors → melanin deposition
  • Visible in skin creases, buccal mucosa, scars
  • In secondary disease, ACTH is low → no hyperpigmentation

Summary: Part 3

  • Cushing’s syndrome results from glucocorticoid excess — iatrogenic, adrenal, or ectopic ACTH
  • Clinical features reflect the exaggerated physiological actions of cortisol across all systems
  • Addison’s disease results from cortisol deficiency — primary (adrenal) or secondary (pituitary)
  • Hyperpigmentation distinguishes primary from secondary disease via the POMC/melanocortin pathway
  • Addisonian crisis (hypotension + hyperkalaemia) is a medical emergency
  • Both conditions are treatable but require lifelong management

Self-Assessment

Q1: A patient presents with cortisol excess, elevated ACTH, and no pituitary abnormality on MRI. What is the most likely diagnosis? Ectopic ACTH syndrome — a non-pituitary tumour (e.g., small cell lung carcinoma, bronchial carcinoid) is producing ACTH autonomously. The elevated ACTH drives adrenal cortisol production, but the source is outside the pituitary.
Q2: Why does secondary adrenal insufficiency NOT cause hyperpigmentation? In secondary insufficiency, the problem is at the pituitary or hypothalamus, so ACTH production is reduced, not elevated. Since hyperpigmentation is caused by ACTH-related peptides activating melanocortin receptors, low ACTH means no excess melanin deposition.
Q3: Explain the mechanism behind hyperkalaemia in Addison’s disease. The adrenal cortex also produces aldosterone (zona glomerulosa). In primary adrenal insufficiency, aldosterone production is impaired alongside cortisol. Aldosterone normally promotes renal potassium excretion and sodium retention. Its absence leads to potassium retention (hyperkalaemia) and sodium/water loss (hypotension).
